Step 01

The Arbitrage Model — How You Profit From Property You Don't Own

Rental arbitrage is simple: you sign a normal long-term lease on a property with the landlord's written permission to sublet, furnish it, and list it on Airbnb and VRBO at nightly rates. The spread between what guests pay you and what you pay the landlord is your profit.

Nightly rate ($135) × 20 booked nights+ $2,700
Cleaning fees collected (6 turnovers × $90)+ $540
Rent to landlord− $1,200
Utilities + Wi-Fi− $260
Cleaning crew payout (6 × $90)− $540
Supplies, software, insurance− $190
Monthly profit — one unit$1,050

That example is a modest mid-market unit at 65% occupancy. Run five units like that and you've replaced a full-time income — with no mortgage, no down payment, and no property tax bill.

The golden rule: Arbitrage only works when the landlord knows and approves in writing. Sneaking Airbnb guests into a lease that forbids subletting gets you evicted, sued, and banned from the platform. The pitch in Step 4 exists so you never have to hide anything.

Step 02

Market Research — Pick Winners Before You Sign Anything

Most arbitrage failures happen here, before a single guest checks in. People sign leases in markets where the nightly rates can't cover rent. Twenty minutes of research prevents a 12-month mistake.

The 2X Rule

A unit qualifies only if projected monthly Airbnb revenue is at least 2× the monthly rent. $1,200 rent needs $2,400+ in projected revenue. Below that, occupancy dips and slow seasons will eat you alive.

Research tools

AirDNA — Revenue Data → Rabbu — Free Calculator → Mashvisor → Airbnb — Manual Comps →

The Free Manual Method (works without paying for anything)

Open Airbnb, search your target neighborhood with dates 3–4 weeks out. Filter to the bedroom count you're considering. Now study the top 10 listings: nightly rates, how many dates are blocked on their calendars (blocked = booked), review counts, and amenities. If the top listings are 70%+ booked at $120+/night and rent runs $1,100, you've found a market.

Check the law BEFORE you fall in love with a unit

Every city treats short-term rentals differently. Some require a simple registration, some cap the number of permits, some ban non-owner-occupied STRs entirely. Search "[your city] short-term rental ordinance" and call the city clerk's office — they answer these questions every day.

Airbnb — Responsible Hosting Rules by Area →
Red flags that kill a market: permit caps already maxed out, HOA-heavy neighborhoods, college towns with 9-month demand, and any city actively suing Airbnb hosts. One phone call to the clerk saves you thousands.

What makes a unit guest-magnetic

  • Location score: within 15 minutes of a hospital, university, stadium, downtown, or major employer. Hospitals are gold — traveling nurses book 30+ day stays.
  • 1BR and 2BR units hit the sweet spot of rent-to-rate ratio. Studios cap your rate; 4BRs cap your guest pool.
  • In-unit washer/dryer, dedicated parking, ground floor or elevator — the three amenities that move bookings most in mid-size markets.
  • Newer or recently renovated — photos sell the listing, and dated interiors can't be photographed around.
Step 03

Finding Units — The Zillow Landlord Method + 6 More Pipelines

Your deal flow is everything. Here are seven pipelines, starting with the highest-converting one: going direct to landlords on Zillow.

Pipeline #1 — The Zillow Direct-to-Landlord Method

Step-by-step

  • 1. Go to Zillow Rentals and search your target ZIP codes.
  • 2. Filter: your price range, 1–2 BR, and sort by "Newest." Fresh listings mean motivated landlords.
  • 3. Identify private landlords vs. big property managers. Listings with phone numbers, slightly amateur photos, or "listed by property owner" are your targets. Small landlords say yes far more often than corporate leasing offices.
  • 4. Look for listings sitting 30+ days — that landlord is losing money every day and is far more open to a creative tenant who guarantees rent.
  • 5. Contact through the listing, but always push to get them on the phone. The pitch (Step 4) converts on calls, not in Zillow's message box.
  • 6. Track every contact in a simple spreadsheet: address, rent, landlord name, date contacted, response, follow-up date. Follow up at day 3 and day 7. Most yeses come on the follow-up.

Step 04

The Landlord Pitch — Word-for-Word Scripts That Get the Yes

Landlords don't care about your business. They care about three things: guaranteed rent, a protected property, and zero headaches. Every script below sells those three things — never the word "Airbnb" in the first sentence.

Script — First Phone Call

"Hi, my name's [Name] — I'm calling about your unit on [Street]. I run a small hospitality company here in [city], and I'm looking for a long-term lease, twelve months minimum. Here's what makes me different from a normal tenant: I'd be using the unit to host vetted, short-stay guests — mostly traveling professionals — so the rent doesn't depend on my paycheck, it's backed by my business. You get rent on the first, every month, guaranteed, whether the unit is booked or not. I also carry a commercial insurance policy on top of your coverage and the place gets professionally cleaned multiple times a week. Could I walk the unit this week and show you how I protect the property?"

Script — Zillow / Email Message

"Hi [Name], I'm interested in your listing at [address]. Quick context so I'm not wasting your time: I operate furnished short-stay housing for traveling professionals (nurses, contractors, relocating families). I'm looking for a 12–24 month lease, I pay rent on the 1st regardless of occupancy, I carry $1M commercial liability insurance beyond your landlord policy, and the unit gets professional cleaning 2–3x weekly — so it's maintained better than a typical rental. Happy to provide references, proof of insurance, and walk you through exactly how I screen guests. Are you open to a 10-minute call this week?"

Script — Text Follow-Up (Day 3)

"Hi [Name], this is [Name] — reached out about [address] earlier this week. I know 'short-stay housing' can sound unfamiliar, so here's the short version: you get guaranteed rent on the 1st, a unit that's professionally cleaned more often than any normal rental, and an extra $1M insurance policy protecting your property. Worst case for you is a tenant who pays on time and keeps the place spotless. Still happy to chat — when's good?"

Sweeteners that close stubborn landlords

  • Offer $50–$150/month above asking rent. Sounds backwards, but if the unit clears the 2X rule, paying a small premium to lock in a yes is the cheapest customer acquisition you'll ever do.
  • Longer lease term — offer 18–24 months. Landlords value vacancy elimination above almost everything.
  • Larger security deposit — 1.5–2x deposit signals you're not worried about damage, which tells them they shouldn't be either.
  • Quarterly walk-through rights — invite them to inspect anytime with notice. Transparency kills fear.
  • Revenue share option — for sophisticated landlords: base rent slightly below asking + 10–15% of revenue above a threshold. Now they're rooting for your bookings.
Always close with paper: Once they're verbally in, send a one-page summary — lease term, rent, your insurance, cleaning schedule, guest screening rules, and the sublease permission clause. Professional paper turns a "maybe" into a signature.
Step 05

Furnishing Without Draining Your Pocket — 5 Plays

Furnishing is the biggest startup cost in arbitrage — $3,500–$8,000 for a one-bedroom done right. Here's how to push that cost onto the property owner, a finance company, or the calendar instead of your bank account.

Play #1 — Get the Owner to Furnish It (the ask most people never make)

Frame it as an investment in their asset, not a favor to you: "If you furnish the unit, I'll sign at $150/month above asking on a 24-month lease — that's $3,600 extra to you, the furniture is yours and stays with your property forever, and your unit becomes a furnished rental worth 15–25% more rent to any future tenant after me." You can also offer a furnishing allowance structure: owner spends up to $5,000 on furniture (you provide the exact shopping list), and you repay it through a $200/month rent bump. They earn interest, keep the furniture, and you keep your cash.

Play #2 — Target Already-Furnished Units

Filter Zillow and Furnished Finder for "furnished" listings. Estate situations, relocating owners, and failed-Airbnb landlords (huge opportunity — they already have the furniture AND understand the model) list fully furnished units constantly. A failed STR host's unit is the perfect arbitrage target: furnished, permitted, and owned by someone who already believes in short-term rental income but didn't want to operate it.

Play #3 — Furniture Rental / Rent-to-Own

Furniture rental companies will furnish an entire 1BR for roughly $200–$400/month with no upfront capital — and it's a deductible operating expense. Use it to launch, then replace pieces with owned furniture as revenue comes in.

CORT Furniture Rental → Brook Furniture Rental → Fernish →

Play #4 — 0% Financing + Secondhand Hybrid

Big-ticket new items (mattresses, sofa) on 0% promotional financing through store cards or buy-now-pay-later; everything else secondhand. Facebook Marketplace, estate sales, and hotel liquidators are goldmines — hotel liquidation outlets sell commercial-grade furniture built for exactly your use case at 50–70% off retail.

Hotel Surplus Outlet → EstateSales.net →

Play #5 — The Mid-Term First, Upgrade Later Strategy

Launch as a 30+ day furnished rental for traveling nurses on Furnished Finder with basic furnishing (~$2,500), bank 2–3 months of profit, then upgrade the décor and switch to nightly Airbnb rates. You start earning with the cheap setup and let the unit pay for its own glow-up.

Step 06

Legal Protection & Insurance — Build the Moat

This section is what separates a real operator from someone playing landlord roulette. Four layers of protection, in order:

Layer 1 — The LLC

Form an LLC before you sign your first lease, and sign the lease in the LLC's name where the landlord allows it. It separates your personal assets from the business, looks more professional in the pitch, and sets up clean bookkeeping. File directly with your Secretary of State (usually $40–$150) — you don't need an expensive service.

IRS — Free EIN → WV Secretary of State → KY Secretary of State →

Layer 2 — The Sublease Addendum (the document that makes this legal)

Your lease must contain explicit written permission. A one-paragraph addendum signed by both parties does it. The clause should state:

What the addendum must cover

  • Tenant (your LLC) is expressly authorized to operate the premises as furnished short-term lodging and sublet to guests
  • Tenant maintains commercial liability insurance of at least $1M naming landlord as additional insured
  • Tenant is responsible for all STR permits, registrations, and local tax compliance
  • Tenant responsible for guest-caused damage beyond normal wear
  • Landlord inspection rights with 24–48 hr notice

Have a local real estate attorney review your template once ($200–$400) — then reuse it on every deal. That's the cheapest legal protection you'll ever buy.

Layer 3 — Real STR Insurance (Airbnb's AirCover is NOT insurance)

AirCover is a damage reimbursement program with exclusions, not a liability policy. You need actual commercial short-term rental insurance:

Proper Insurance → Safely → Steadily →

Layer 4 — Permits & Taxes

  • City STR registration/permit — whatever your ordinance requires; the cost goes in your startup budget and the responsibility goes in your lease addendum.
  • Lodging/occupancy tax — Airbnb collects and remits in many jurisdictions, but verify what YOUR city and state require. Search "[state] transient occupancy tax."
  • Track everything — every furnishing receipt, mile driven, and supply run is deductible. Get a bookkeeper or at minimum a dedicated business bank account from day one.

The cleaner system that protects your ratings

Your cleaner is your eyes on the property. Require: (1) photo checklist after every turnover in Turno, (2) immediate damage reports with photos — that's how you claim against guest deposits in time, (3) restocking duties built into the rate (toilet paper, coffee, soap), and (4) a backup cleaner who's done the unit at least once. Step 10

Scaling — From One Unit to a Portfolio

  • Stabilize before you multiply. Unit #1 should run 60+ days at target occupancy with systems doing the work before you sign unit #2. Your profit from unit #1 funds the furnishing of unit #2 — that's the flywheel.
  • Go back to your landlord first. The easiest second unit is from the landlord you already pay on the 1st. "Do you have anything else coming vacant — or do you know other owners?" Small landlords all know each other.
  • Standardize everything. Same furniture package, same supply list, same listing template, same cleaner checklist. Unit #4 should take half the setup time of unit #1.
  • Watch the 3 numbers weekly: occupancy rate, average nightly rate, and revenue per unit vs. its 2X threshold. A unit that slips under 1.5X for two straight months gets repriced, repositioned (mid-term nurse housing), or non-renewed.
  • The exit ramps: arbitrage profits become down payments. Plenty of operators run 5–10 arbitrage units, then start buying — you already know exactly which properties cash flow because you've been operating them.
The whole model in one sentence: You're not in the property business — you're in the hospitality business, renting the real estate wholesale and selling it retail by the night, with the landlord's blessing and other people doing the turnovers.
